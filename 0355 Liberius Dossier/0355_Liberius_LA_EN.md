| **2. 1.** Quamuis sub imagine pacis humani generis inimicus uehementius in membra ecclesiae uideatur esse grassatus, uos tamen [[p. 165]] acceptissimos deo sacerdotes egregia et singularis fides et hic probabiles deo ostendit et iam ad futuram gloriam martyres designauit. quo itaque praeconio laudis, qua uocis exultatione merita uirtutis uestrae praeferam, positus inter merorem absentiae uestrae et gaudium gloriae prorsus inuenire non possum, nisi quod scio hic me probabiliora uobis solatia exhibere, si credatis me in exilio simul uobiscum esse detrusum. denique me in ipsa adhuc expectatione pendentem, quod a consortio uestro durior necessitas interim detrahit, satis contristor. optaueram enim, fratres deuotissimi, prior pro omnibus uobis impendi, ut exemplum gloriae per me magis uestra dilectio consequeretur. sed fuerit haec palma meritorum uestrorum, ut priores de perseuerantia fidei ad confessionis inlustrem gloriam ueniretis. quaeso igitur dilectionem uestram, ut me praesentem uobiscum [cum] esse credatis atque eo me affectu absentem non esse sentiatis et intellegatis dolere me satis, quod sim interim a uestro consortio disparatus. quantam denique gloriam fueritis consecuti, hinc magis scire potestis, quod, si qui in persecutione coronati sunt, solius persecutoris cruentos gladios sentire potuerunt, contra uos, deuoti per omnia dei milites, etiam falsos fratres inimicos experti uictoriam de perfidis pertulistis, quorum, quantumque in saeculo uiolentia increscere potuit, tanto [[p. 166]] sacerdotibus praemia laudis inueniuntur conferre. estote itaque de promissione caelesti securi. | | **2. 1.** Although, under the appearance of peace, the enemy of the human race seems to have raged the more violently against the members of the Church, yet you — priests most acceptable to God — an outstanding and singular faith has both shown to be approved in God's sight here and now, and has already marked out as martyrs for the glory to come. With what heralding of praise, then, with what exultation of voice I might set forth the merits of your virtue, placed as I am between grief at your absence and joy at your glory, I simply cannot discover — except that I know I offer you here a more convincing consolation, if you believe that I have been thrust into exile together with you. In short, I am grieved enough that I am left still hanging in expectation, because a harsher necessity meanwhile withdraws me from your fellowship. For I had wished, most devoted brothers, to be spent first on behalf of you all, so that through me your love might the more attain an example of glory. But let this be the palm of your merits — that you should come first, by your perseverance in the faith, to the illustrious glory of confession. I ask your love, therefore, to believe me present with you, and so to feel by that affection that I am not absent, and to understand that I grieve enough that I am for the time being separated from your fellowship. And how great a glory you have attained you can better know from this: that whereas those who were crowned in persecution could feel only the bloody swords of the persecutor, you, God's soldiers devoted in all things, having found even false brethren for enemies, carried off the victory over the faithless; and in proportion as their violence could grow in the world, in that same measure rewards of praise are found to be conferred on the priests. Be therefore secure concerning the heavenly promise. |

And because I long to know more faithfully what was done in that very engagement, I beseech your holiness: deign to make it all known to me faithfully by letter, so that either my very spirit, which is tortured by conflicting rumours, or the strength of my body itself, which is already worn thin, may find some further support from your encouragement. *And in another hand:* May God keep you unharmed, my lord brothers.  |

| DISPVTATIO IMPERATORIS CONSTANTII ET LIBERII ROMANI PONTIFICIS. | | DISPUTATION OF THE EMPEROR CONSTANTIUS AND LIBERIUS, ROMAN PONTIFF. |
| **2.** Constantius imperator dixit: 'Nos, et eo quod Christianus sis et episcopus nostrae civitatis, dignum hoc iudicavimus, ut evocantes te moneremus, quatenus communionem scelestissimi Athanasii et ineffabili superbia detenti abnegare non differas. Hoc enim terrarum orbis bene fieri conprobavit, et synodali sententia alienum ecclesiastica communione decrevit.' | | **2.** The emperor Constantius said: 'We — both because you are a Christian and are bishop of our city — have judged it fitting to summon you and admonish you not to delay renouncing the communion of the most wicked Athanasius, held fast by ineffable pride. For the whole world has approved that this be well done, and by a synodal sentence has decreed him an alien from ecclesiastical communion.' |
| **3.** Liberius episcopus dixit: 'O imperator, iudicia ecclesiastica decet cum maxima proferri iustitia. Quapropter, si videtur pietati tuae, disceptationem iube constitui, et, si apparuerit Athanasius condemnatione dignus, tunc secundum ecclesiasticae consequentiae formam profertur in eum digna sententia. Non enim possibile est condemnare virum, de quo nihil examinatum est.' | | **3.** Bishop Liberius said: 'O emperor, ecclesiastical judgments ought to be delivered with the utmost justice. Wherefore, if it seem good to your piety, order a hearing to be established; and if Athanasius appears worthy of condemnation, then, according to the form of ecclesiastical procedure, a fitting sentence is pronounced against him. For it is not possible to condemn a man about whom nothing has been examined.' |
| **4.** Constantius imperator dixit: 'Universus mundus adiudicavit eius facinora; sed ille, sicut ab initio, tempus illudit.' | | **4.** The emperor Constantius said: 'The whole world has adjudged his crimes; but he, as from the beginning, mocks the time.' |
| [[p. 238]] **5.** Liberius episcopus dixit: 'Quicumque suscripserunt, non ipsi viderunt, quae gesta sunt; sed propter gloriam et timorem et, ne a te contumelias paterentur, adquieverunt.' | | [[p. 238]] **5.** Bishop Liberius said: 'Whoever subscribed did not themselves see what was done; but for the sake of glory and out of fear, and lest they suffer insults from you, they acquiesced.' |
| **6.** Imperator dixit: 'Quid est gloria et timor et contumeliae?' | | **6.** The emperor said: 'What is this glory and fear and insults?' |
| **7.** Liberius dixit: 'Quicumque gloriam dei non diligunt, tua magis dona praeponunt. Quem ergo isti non viderunt, non examinantes adiudicaverunt, quod est Christianitatis alienum.' | | **7.** Liberius said: 'Whoever do not love the glory of God prefer your gifts. Him, therefore, whom these men did not see, they adjudged without examining — which is alien to Christianity.' |
| **8.** Imperator dixit: 'In facie utique condemnatus est in concilio Tyri et in concilio contra eum pariter decreverunt omnes totius orbis episcopi.' | | **8.** The emperor said: 'He was assuredly condemned to his face in the council of Tyre, and in the council all the bishops of the whole world alike decreed against him.' |
| **9.** Liberius dixit: 'Numquam in praesentia sui homo ille adiudicatus est. Quicumque enim tunc convenientes condemnaverunt eum, discedente Athanasio a iudicio sententiam protulerunt.' | | **9.** Liberius said: 'That man was never judged in his own presence. For whoever then assembled and condemned him pronounced sentence after Athanasius had withdrawn from the tribunal.' |
| **10.** Eusebius eunuchus dixit: 'In Nicaeno concilio alienus catholica fide probatus est.' | | **10.** Eusebius the eunuch said: 'In the council of Nicaea he was proved an alien from the catholic faith.' |
| **11.** Liberius dixit: 'Quinque soli iudicaverunt ex his, qui cum eo navigio venerunt in Mareote, quos miserunt ad accusationem eius, ut contra eum gesta conficerent. Horum itaque, qui destinati sunt, duo sunt mortui, Theognius et Theodorus; reliqui vero tres vivunt, hoc est Maris, Valens et Ursacius. Ab his, qui missi sunt, Serdicae propter hanc causam synodus celebrata est. Qui in synodo libellos obtulerunt veniam postulantes, quoniam in Mareotem per calumniam ex una parte contra Athanasium gesta confecerunt. Libellos eorum nunc prae manibus habemus. [[p. 239]] Cui horum nos credere et communicare convenit, imperator, his, qui primitus damnaverunt et veniam postea poposcerunt, an eis, qui adhuc haec accusare contendunt?' | | **11.** Liberius said: 'Only five judged, of those who came with him by ship to the Mareotis, whom they sent for his accusation, to draw up proceedings against him. Of these, then, who were sent out, two are dead, Theognius and Theodorus; but the remaining three are alive, that is, Maris, Valens and Ursacius. By these men who were sent, a synod was held at Serdica for this cause. And they in the synod presented petitions asking pardon, because in the Mareotis they had, by calumny and from one side only, drawn up proceedings against Athanasius. Their petitions we now have in hand. [[p. 239]] To which of these does it befit us to give credence and communion, emperor — to those who first condemned him and afterward begged pardon, or to those who still strive to press this accusation?' |
| **12.** Epictetus episcopus dixit: 'O imperator, non fidei causa hodie neque iudiciorum ecclesiasticorum sermonem facit, sed ut apud senatores in Romana constitutos urbe glorietur, quasi superaverit principem.' | | **12.** Bishop Epictetus said: 'O emperor, he is making his speech today not for the cause of faith nor of ecclesiastical judgments, but that he may boast before the senators established in the city of Rome, as though he had overcome the prince.' |
| **13.** Imperator ad Liberium dixit: 'Quota pars es totius orbis, quoniam tu solus hominem vindicas nefandissimum et orbis terrarum pacem ecclesiaeque dissolvis?' | | **13.** The emperor said to Liberius: 'What fraction are you of the whole world, that you alone champion a most abominable man and dissolve the peace of the world and of the Church?' |
| **14.** Liberius dixit: 'Non eo quod ego sum solus, fidei causa minuitur. Nam et antiquitus tres soli reperti sunt resistentes praecepto regis.' | | **14.** Liberius said: 'It is not because I am alone that the cause of faith is lessened. For in antiquity too only three were found resisting the king's command.' |
| **15.** Eusebius eunuchus dixit: 'Imperatorem nostrum Nabuchodonosor fecisti?' | | **15.** Eusebius the eunuch said: 'Have you made our emperor a Nebuchadnezzar?' |
| **16.** Liberius dixit: 'Non. Sed ita inrationabiliter adiudicandus est homo, qui non est examinatus? Haec ego peto primitus generalem proferri suscriptionem confirmantem fidem Nicaea prolatam, ut ita revocatis ab exilio fratribus nostris et in locis propriis restitutis, si hi, qui turbas pariunt ecclesiis, visi fuerint apostolicae fidei consentire, tunc apud Alexandriam convenientes omnes, ubi et accusatus et accusatores sunt et defensor eorum, examinata causa possimus proferre sententiam.' | | **16.** Liberius said: 'No. But is a man to be adjudged so irrationally who has not been examined? This I ask first: that a general subscription be brought forth confirming the faith set out at Nicaea; so that thus, our brothers being recalled from exile and restored to their own places, if those who breed disturbances for the churches shall be seen to consent to the apostolic faith, then all assembling at Alexandria — where both the accused and the accusers and their defender are — having examined the cause, we may be able to pronounce sentence.' |
| **17.** Epictetus episcopus dixit: 'Sed cursus publicus non sufficit subvectui episcoporum.' | | **17.** Bishop Epictetus said: 'But the public post does not suffice for the conveyance of the bishops.' |
| **18.** Liberius dixit: 'Ecclesiasticae causae publico cursu non [[p. 240]] egent. Ecclesiae namque sufficiunt usque ad mare perducere suos episcopos.' | | **18.** Liberius said: 'Ecclesiastical causes have no need [[p. 240]] of the public post. For the churches suffice to bring their own bishops as far as the sea.' |
| **19.** Imperator dixit: 'Quae dudum susceperunt formam, solvi non possunt; plurimorum namque episcoporum debet valere sententia. Tu solus es, qui amicitias illius scelerati defendis.' | | **19.** The emperor said: 'What things have long since received their settled form cannot be undone; for the sentence of very many bishops ought to prevail. You alone are the one who defends the friendships of that criminal.' |
| **20.** Liberius dixit: 'Imperator, numquam audivimus absente accusato iudicem eius scelera proferentem, nisi quia propria videntur haec odia.' | | **20.** Liberius said: 'Emperor, we have never heard of a judge, in the absence of the accused, setting forth his crimes — unless because these hatreds appear to be personal.' |
| **21.** Imperator dixit: 'Omnibus equidem nocuit in communione, nulli tamen ita sicuti mihi. Cui dum non sufficeret interitus fratris mei maioris, neque beatae memoriae Constantem cessavit in meis inimicitiis frequenter impellere, nisi nos maiori mansuetudine detenti impellentis momenta temperassemus. Nulla itaque mihi victoria est, neque contra Magnentium vel Silvanum, nisi illum scelestum ab ecclesiasticis rebus expulero.' | | **21.** The emperor said: 'He has indeed harmed all in the communion, yet none so much as me. For when the destruction of my elder brother did not satisfy him, he did not cease frequently to drive even Constans, of blessed memory, into enmities against me, until we, restrained by the greater gentleness, tempered the impulses of the one driving him on. And so I have no victory — neither against Magnentius nor against Silvanus — unless I shall have expelled that wicked man from ecclesiastical affairs.' |
| **22.** Liberius dixit: 'Noli per episcopos vindicare tuas inimicitias; manus enim ecclesiasticorum ad sanctificandum vacare debent. Quapropter, si tibi videtur, iube vocari ad loca propria sacerdotes, et, si concordes apparuerint ei, quem nos in praesenti defendimus secundum fidem orthodoxam in Nicaena synodo constitutam, tunc convenientes providebunt pro mundi pace, ut homo, qui non peccavit, non reprobetur iniuste.' | | **22.** Liberius said: 'Do not avenge your enmities through bishops; for the hands of churchmen ought to be free for sanctifying. Wherefore, if it seem good to you, order the priests to be summoned to their own places; and if they appear to be in concord with him whom we for the present defend according to the orthodox faith established in the Nicene synod, then, assembling, they will provide for the peace of the world, so that a man who has not sinned may not be unjustly condemned.' |
| **23.** Imperator dixit: 'Unum est, quod quaeritur: volo enim te amplectentem ecclesiasticam communionem Romae denuo destinare; et propterea concorda paci et suscribens Romae revertere.' | | **23.** The emperor said: 'One thing only is sought: for I wish to send you back to Rome, embracing the ecclesiastical communion; and therefore be reconciled to peace and, subscribing, return to Rome.' |
| [[p. 241]] **24.** Liberius dixit: 'Romae iam fratribus valedixi; maiores enim sunt ecclesiasticae sanctiones quam Romanae habitatio civitatis.' | | [[p. 241]] **24.** Liberius said: 'At Rome I have already said farewell to the brethren; for the sanctions of the Church are greater than dwelling in the city of Rome.' |
| **25.** Imperator dixit: 'Ergo trium dierum spatium ad deliberationem habes; si vis suscribere, Romae revertere aut certe cogita, in quem vis deportari locum.' | | **25.** The emperor said: 'You have, then, the space of three days for deliberation; if you are willing to subscribe, return to Rome; or else consider to what place you wish to be deported.' |
| **26.** Liberius dixit: 'Spatium trium dierum rationem nequaquam mutare potest. Quapropter, ubi vis, dirige me.' | | **26.** Liberius said: 'The space of three days can in no way change my reasoning. Wherefore, send me where you will.' |
| **27.** Imperator autem post duos dies examinato Liberio et nequaquam a suo propositu revocato iussit eum in Beroea **28.** Thraciae exilio deportari. Egredienti Liberio imperator ad expensas quingentos solidos destinavit. Tunc Liberius deferenti dixit: 'Vade, da eos imperatori; opus enim habet **29.** suis militibus erogare.' Similiter et Augusta tantos ei direxit. Liberius dixit: 'Redde haec imperatori; opus enim habet haec ad expensas militum suorum. Si autem imperator haec non habet opus, det eos Auxentio et Epicteto; illi enim opus haec habent.' **30.** Dumque ab eis nihil accepisset, Eusebius eunuchus obtulit ei alios solidos. Cui Liberius ait: 'Totius orbis ecclesias desolasti et tamquam damnato mihi haec afferis? Vade, prius esse imbuere Christianus.' His gestis post tres dies nihil accipiens missus exilio est. | | **27.** But the emperor, after two days, Liberius having been examined and in no way recalled from his purpose, ordered him to be deported into exile at Beroea **28.** in Thrace. As Liberius was departing, the emperor assigned five hundred solidi for expenses. Then Liberius said to the man who brought them: 'Go, give them to the emperor; for he needs them **29.** to pay out to his soldiers.' Likewise the Augusta sent him as much. Liberius said: 'Give these back to the emperor; for he needs them for the expenses of his soldiers. But if the emperor has no need of them, let him give them to Auxentius and Epictetus; for they have need of them.' **30.** And when he had taken nothing from them, Eusebius the eunuch offered him other solidi. To whom Liberius said: 'You have laid waste the churches of the whole world, and do you bring these to me as to one condemned? Go, be first instructed to be a Christian.' These things done, after three days, taking nothing, he was sent into exile.  |
