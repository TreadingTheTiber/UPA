- *Selection: the propositions as denounced; the full fourth conclusion (D5); Peña's case-note; Peña's Latin censura; Peña to the royal confessor (D3); the Roman commission's censure (D7)*

*Note on this selection. These are the founding documents of the dispute that runs through this whole repository. Chronology: in July 1601 Gaspar Hurtado, then regent of Arts at Alcalá (later a Jesuit, and successor of Gabriel Vázquez in the chair of Prima), defended in one of his nine doctoral acts, under Álvaro de Villegas, printed conclusions whose fourth conclusion — the act's official question was on the Mosaic bill of divorce — contained the two propositions here documented (the printed sheet is from October/November 1601). The sheet reached Clement VIII on 2 March 1602; on 7 March 1602 Jesuits defended the thesis again at Alcalá; the Spanish Inquisition intervened (whether anyone was actually imprisoned was later denied by Hurtado himself); Báñez held his clarifying act at Valladolid on 2 July 1602 (see the 1602 Báñez entry), the Jesuits their counter-act on 10 July; and on 19–20 July 1602 a commission of theologians convened by special commission of Clement VIII at the palace of the Roman Inquisition unanimously censured four propositions excerpted from printed Alcalá conclusions (the note signed, per Peña, on 23 July).*

*On the commission's authority: it was a formally constituted qualification panel — papal commission, Holy Office venue, propositions consigned to the Commissary General by papal mandate, unanimous vote, and signatories including the Dominican Master General (Xavierre), the Master of the Sacred Palace, the Commissary General of the Inquisition, the Franciscan general Juan de Rada, Diego Álvarez, and Michelangelo Seghizzi (the later Commissary of the Galileo injunction of 1616). But it is a consultors' censura feeding a process, not a promulgated condemnation: it survives only in manuscript, no public decree followed, and the accused went free.*

*On its bearing on the question of this repository: the four censured propositions form a system beneath the famous thesis. P1 (divine faith conditioned on a necessary human assent) makes every concrete application of faith inherit fallibility at the human joint — censured as an error in faith savoring of (Semi-)Pelagianism. P2 (prudential knowledge suffices for the Church's grasp of the revelation entrusted to her) is the Alcalá epistemology generalized to the whole Church, rendering her recognition of canon, councils, and her living rule of faith fallible in principle — censured flatly as scandalous, pernicious, and heretical, with Rada adding that in its exclusive sense it is "subversive of the whole faith." P3 (Roman succession not established de iure divino, though most probable) degrades the universal premise of the whole syllogism, so that no acceptance however peaceful could yield more than probability — censured as scandalous, paving the way to schism, heretical or at minimum proximate to heresy. P4 (heresy = contradiction of "commonly known" doctrine) is a safe-harbor definition immunizing deniers of non-notorious connected truths — censured as temerarious, scandalous, strewing the way to the defense of errors. What the commission never censured is the headline thesis itself — "non est de fide hunc numero Papam esse verum Papam" is absent from the four propositions. Rome condemned every route by which the Church's acceptance-judgment could be rendered fallible, while leaving the note-question (per se primo vs. per se secundo) untouched as a school dispute — which explains how Hurtado could later write (1632; see that entry) that his doctrine had been "judged completely sound," and why the Salmanticenses and Palanco could truthfully say the negative opinion as such was never condemned. Note also Peña's own position: his censura grounds itself on Báñez's resolution (denial of the accepted pope's legitimacy punishable as heresy) and adds the providence argument — that it belongs supremely to divine providence not to permit an unbaptized man or a woman to ascend to the pontificate — making Peña a substantive witness to the infallibility of the Church's acceptance, and, as Torrijos observes, the first to read Báñez correctly, months before the Jesuits conscripted him for the opposite side.*
