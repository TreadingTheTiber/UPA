# De Romano Pontifice, Liber IV, Capitulum X

**It came to pass shortly thereafter that Sylverius died, and Vigilius, who until then had sat in schism, began now to be the sole and legitimate pontiff, indeed, with the Roman clergy and people confirming and accepting him.** From this time forward, however, neither error nor semblance of error was found in Vigilius, but rather the utmost constancy in faith until death, so that it appears that with the pontificate itself he received firmness of faith, and was transformed from light chaff into most solid rock. For when the heretical Empress Theodora, relying on secret letters and Vigilius's promise, asked him to restore the aforementioned Patriarch Anthemius, as he had promised, he wrote back that he had rashly promised and had gravely sinned by that promise, and therefore could not and would not fulfill what he had promised, lest he should add sins to sins. For this reason, he was sent into exile by the enraged empress and miserably persecuted until death. This is recorded not only in the Pontifical, but also noted by Paul the Deacon in his Life of Justinian, and by Aimonius in book 5, chapter 32, of Deeds of the Franks, and even by the Magdeburg Centuriators themselves in Century 6, chapter 10, in the Life of Vigilius, and the same is also very briefly indicated by Liberatus at the end of chapter 22, where he says that Vigilius was later miserably afflicted by that very heresy which he had secretly fostered in the beginning.

*Note on the De Conciliis addition (2026): this excerpt supplies the engine of what may be called the output-guarantee reading of universal acceptance (the Laymann–Muratori school documented in the 1620 and 1714 entries). Three doctrines: (1) the assistance-principle — the Spirit assists the council "not so much for the sake of the council itself, as for the sake of the universal Church," so that even a council of feigned bishops would be compelled to propose the truest faith: the divine guarantee is aimed at what the Church must receive, not at the credentials of the instruments; (2) yet Bellarmine immediately adds the input-side reassurance — providence will not permit an illegitimate or non-catholic majority to assemble in a council "which is believed by the Church to be legitimate"; (3) the epistemology of legitimacy: for contemporaries the fact is evident ("nothing invisible is required"); for posterity it is "human faith, but most certain, comparable in a manner to natural evidence" — and the closing syllogism (de fide universal + most-certain-human-faith minor → decrees believed certa fide) is exactly the syllogism Muratori would construct for the papal case seventy years later. Bellarmine thus stands at the boundary of the two schools: the conciliar assistance-principle grounds School 2, while the Vigilius narrative of De Rom. Pont. IV.10 (this entry's other excerpt: Vigilius "began now to be the sole and legitimate pontiff, with the Roman clergy and people confirming and accepting him") and the providence-over-inputs remark lean toward School 1.*

## De Conciliis, Book II, Chapter IX (at the end) — Solution of objections: the Holy Spirit's assistance for the sake of the universal Church

To the other objection I reply that it is not necessary for us to know that the bishops who assemble for a council have true faith and true ordination; it is enough if the contrary is not established. For even if the supreme pontiff (which we believe cannot happen) and all the bishops whom the supreme pontiff convokes to some general council were heretics at heart, Catholics by external profession alone, and had not truly received the invisible character of sacred order but a bare imposition of hands: nevertheless we are certain that God would never permit such a council to err in framing its decrees. **For the Holy Spirit assists the council, not so much for the sake of the council itself, as for the sake of the universal Church, which by divine precept is bound not to dispute the judgment of the bishops, but to venerate it. And so, lest the universal Church should err, divine providence would bring it about that even that council of feigned bishops would, willing or unwilling, propose the truest faith for the Church to follow.** Accordingly, in order that some assembly of bishops be, and be called, legitimate, this alone is required: that those bishops assemble who ordinarily preside in the Church, and that they be openly held by all to be true and catholic bishops — whatever may be the case with the things that lie hidden in their hearts.

Since, however, we not only know that God has the salvation of His Church at heart, but have also learned from the Scriptures that God's providence disposes all things sweetly: **we therefore believe with certainty that God would not permit that, in a council which is believed by the Church to be legitimate, the bishops assembled — either all of them or the greater part — should be not legitimate, or not catholic.**

[...] That some council is legitimate is evident, without further proof, to those who are present at the time. For they see that bishops have assembled who are held by all to be true bishops, and that they have come together from various provinces, and were called by him who has the authority; and if anything else is required for a legitimate council, this too they can see; for nothing invisible, as we said above, is required. To those who come after, however, or to the absent, **this same thing is known by human faith — but a most certain human faith, which can in some manner be compared to natural evidence.** For example, that the council of Nicaea was legitimate is attested not by one or another historian only, but by many and various writers of its own time and of the times following; and the Church which now exists asserts the very same, nor is there, nor has there been, anyone who thinks or has thought the contrary.

Wherefore, just as that Cicero was once consul, that Julius Caesar was dictator, that Octavian fought a naval battle with Antony, and other things of this kind, although they depend on human faith, are nevertheless so certain that they seem to possess a kind of evidence: so also the councils of the Church which we say were legitimate have so many testimonies of every age that they leave no doubt in our minds against believing that they were such as they are said to be. **Wherefore, since we hold from catholic faith that legitimate councils confirmed by the supreme pontiff cannot err, and know from natural evidence, or from most certain human faith, that the council of Nicaea, of Constantinople, of Ephesus I, and others of this kind were legitimate and approved by the supreme pontiff, it certainly follows that we believe the decrees of such councils with certain faith.**
