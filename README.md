## Taxonomy: the three schools of Universal Peaceful Acceptance

**UPA *simpliciter*** (the genus): the universal, peaceful acceptance of a claimant by the whole Church has **divinely guaranteed effects — at minimum: (i) the validity and bindingness of his acts and decrees** (jurisdiction supplied, if need be, by God directly — Laymann: "all his acts and decrees will be as valid and binding as if he were the true Pontiff"); **(ii) the veracity and safety of his teaching** — he cannot bind the universal Church to error (Laymann: "nor will God permit him to propose his heresy to the Church ex Cathedra"; Bellarmine/Muratori: Christ "does not fail His Church in necessary matters"); **and (iii) the obligation, under grave sin, of obedience and communion** (Muratori). School 1 adds the fact itself; School 2 stops at these effects; **no school in the tradition stops short of them**. Every source in this repository affirms the genus. The schools divide over *where the guarantee terminates*:

### School 1 — *UPA stricte*: the fact-guarantee

The acceptance infallibly certifies (as **sign**) or effects (as **cause** — the "second election" / *sanatio* theory) **the fact itself**: the whole Church *cannot* peacefully accept a non-pope.

- **1a — held by divine faith immediately** (*de fide per se primo*): the revealed universal ("every man legitimately elected and accepted by the Church is Peter's successor") is revealed *for the sake of* its singulars, so "this man is pope" is itself an object of divine faith. The acceptance enters the act of faith in the **order of proposition**.
- **1b — held by a lesser assent**, in three grades: **(i)** *de fide per se secundo / mediate* — still divine faith (Báñez, the 1602 Valladolid Assertio); **(ii)** *fides ecclesiastica* — the Church's infallible testimony, a distinct habit (the Lennerz–Salaverri school); **(iii)** *theologice certum* / infallible sign with no faith-habit assigned (Franzelin's th. XIII, Hervé's *signum infallibile*). All of 1b affirms the same counterfactual as 1a: the acceptance **cannot be wrong**.

*A note on Cano (1563):* his own texts supply the machinery (the magistrates-reductio, the canonization providence, the Church holding Eugene IV for true pope) but never state the papal thesis; the classification **1b-i** rests on the testimony of his student Báñez (1602 Relación): "they are deceived who say that he absolutely affirms *non esse de fide hunc hominem... esse summum Pontificem*, for without any doubt he speaks *de fide immediate*... but that they denied it to be *de fide saltem mediate*, I do not believe." Read through Báñez, Cano denied only the immediate mode — the reading this repository adopts.

### School 2 — *UPA functionaliter*: the effect-guarantee (the Laymann–Muratori school)

The fact remains contingently fallible (moral certainty only; the Paul IV discovery-case stays theoretically open), but the **effects** are divinely guaranteed. The school has a real lineage in this repository: **Gregory of Valencia** (†1603; the 1619 entry) stands at its head — but in a class of his own (labeled **2→1a** in the table). For the *election and acceptance alone* he is School 2: "not from this fact alone — that someone has been elected by the Church to the place of Saint Peter — is it certain with infallible faith that he is the true Pontiff, since one incapable of that office could be elected." But the moment the man *exercises the supreme power*, the guarantee attaches to the fact itself: "as soon as he who has been elected Pontiff establishes something concerning faith or morals that pertains to the universal Church, **by that very fact it is certain to us as a matter of faith that he is a legitimate Pontiff**" — a certitude drawn "from two propositions of faith." The exercise of the office thus converts moral certainty of the title into de fide knowledge of it: an exercise-guarantee unique in the tradition (Suárez cites him as a de fide ally while conceding his "proof is not very conclusive"; Tanner notes the restriction "provided that such a Pontiff has defined something"). The pure effect-guarantee line then runs through **Laymann** (1620), **Pirhing** (1674, via the Lex Barbarius), and **Muratori** (1714); **Tanner** (1627) voices its effects-claim while holding the fact-guarantee, and **Haunold** (1668) rejects the school by name (Lessius and Tanner: "God supplying the defect for a pseudo-pope" is insufficient). The two mechanisms: **(i) juridical supply** — "God, who does not fail in necessities, will supply the defect, and all his acts and decrees will be as valid and binding as if he were the true Pontiff" (Laymann); **(ii) doctrinal providence** — the Bellarmine assistance-principle (*"Assistit enim concilio Spiritus sanctus, non tam propter ipsum concilium, quam propter Ecclesiam universam"*) generalized by Muratori: Christ "does not fail His Church in necessary matters," no ruinous ex cathedra output is possible, and the moral evidence *obliges under grave sin* — its denial "mediately injures" the revealed dogma of special providence. Here the acceptance operates in the **order of execution**: it constitutes the public state that triggers the divine supply, and carries the obliging evidence.

### School 3 — acceptance-nullism: the empty school

The acceptance neither certifies nor protects: the whole Church could adhere to a non-pope *and be harmed by it*. This is what strict sedevacantism requires — and **it has no witness in this repository, nor in the four centuries of the disputed question the repository documents**. Even the strongest critics of the fact-guarantee (Muratori) prosecute the school-3 move — using "it is not *de fide* that this is the true pope" as a warrant for disobedience — as criminal temerity "preluding schism."

**The sedeprivationist variant is School 3 in substance.** The Cassiciacum thesis concedes to the acceptance only a guarantee of *material designation* — that the man accepted is the designated subject who *would* be pope were an obstacle removed — while denying the formal effects: his acts invalid, his magisterium without authority, obedience refusable or forbidden. No theologian in this repository, and none in the tradition it samples, construes the guarantee this way. A "guarantee" that leaves the Church bound to a bare designation while unprotected in doctrine, jurisdiction, and obedience guarantees precisely nothing that any school guaranteed: the School-2 *minimum* — acts "valid and binding **as if he were the true Pontiff**" (Laymann), no error imposable on the universal Church (Bellarmine, Muratori), the title itself certified de fide the moment the man defines (Gregory of Valencia) — is the direct contradictory of a *materialiter-sine-forma* papacy. Relabeling the void does not move the position out of School 3; the census stands: **a designation-only guarantee appears nowhere in the tradition.**

**The "nominal recognition / proximate rule of faith" variant is likewise School 3 in substance.** A newer sedevacantist inversion grants the doctrine (even as ordinary-universal-magisterium teaching), then redefines the *condition*: acceptance, it says, means ongoing submission to the claimant's teaching as "proximate rule of faith," so mass doctrinal resistance since Vatican II shows the conciliar popes were never "accepted" at all. But in all the entries of this repository, the acceptance that certifies is **public adhesion to the man** — communion, obedience de iure, his name in the canon — **completed at accession** (John of St. Thomas: those who "observe that he is not being contradicted are immediately bound to believe that he is the Pope"; Wernz–Vidal: "the sign and infallible effect of a valid election" — certifying *ex tunc*, not a standing plebiscite). Billot's own worked example is Alexander VI against Savonarola: a claim of "most certain proofs" of papal heresy with withheld submission — and *because Christendom adhered, by that very fact the claim is excluded*. Diana's *pacifica* is a completeness-clause at origin (no rival claimant, no divided obedience), which is why 1378–1417 defeats the condition and the uncontested conclaves of 1958–2025 do not. **The challenge stands: name one author who both affirms the acceptance-doctrine and measures the acceptance by continuing doctrinal submission — the census contains none.**

**The theological note of the doctrine itself.** To forestall the "mere theological opinion" dismissal: (a) the **genus** is unanimous across seventeen centuries of witnesses — by *Tuas libenter*'s criteria, ordinary-and-universal-magisterium doctrine in substance, at least infallibly certain; (b) the **fact-guarantee** (School 1) is the common and dominant doctrine, theologically certain, though never solemnly defined — its mere denial is censurable temerity, not demonstrable formal heresy (the 1602 censures were never promulgated; the note-question remained free); (c) the **note of the singular** is freely disputed. The sedevacantist needs (a) to be false, attacks (c), and declares victory — the three grades must not be allowed to collapse into one another, in either direction.

### The classifier test

One counterfactual sorts every author: *Could the whole Church peacefully accept a non-pope?* — School 1: **impossible** (divinely excluded). School 2: **possible but harmless and repairable** (supply meanwhile; declaration upon manifestation). School 3: **possible and catastrophic** (held by no one).

### On *Cum ex apostolatus* (Paul IV, 1559)

The bull — which declares the election of a pre-election heretic null "even if accepted by all with universal obedience for any length of time" — is the strongest apparent proof-text for School 2 (it is Muratori's trump card). But it is a weak prop. The very authors in this repository who face it directly neutralize it in ways that preserve the fact-guarantee: **Passerinus** (1670) reads it under a providential disjunction — either God will so guide the electors and the Church that no heretic is ever elected, *or the defect will be manifested before he is received without opposition by the whole Church*, so the bull's scenario can never intersect a completed peaceful acceptance; **Kirsch** (1767) confines it by its own terms to the fallible *first* election by the cardinals, leaving untouched the *second* election "which derives from the acceptance of the universal Church" (the Pallavicino second-election doctrine, 1649, n. 141); **Cappello** (1912) records it as abrogated law ("formerly there existed the faculty of objection... today, however, no objection whatsoever can be made"). Add Pallavicino's structural point (n. 144): no positive constitution extends, in intention or power, to a case where it would harm the whole community — and the fact that a legislator felt the need to forbid validation-by-acceptance is itself evidence that, absent his law, the acceptance would be understood to validate. A disciplinary bull legislating for a feared hypothesis is not a doctrinal determination that the hypothesis is compatible with God's promises. The same pattern governs the cognate simony-nullity constitutions (Julius II's *Cum tam divino*, Pius IV's *In eligendis*): **Coriolano** (1621) answers that God either prevents such defects or "will immediately reveal them"; **Sylvius** (1628) that once the Church peacefully receives the man, "it is certain by faith that no essential defect intervened"; and Pius X's *Vacante Sede Apostolica* finally abrogated the simony-nullity precisely "that we may remove all pretext for contesting the validity of the election" (quoted in the 1955 Journet entry) — the legislator himself closing the gap the earlier legislators had opened.
